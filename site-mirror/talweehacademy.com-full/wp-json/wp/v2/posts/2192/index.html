Islamic jurisprudence has proved over the centuries that it can adapt to any time or place, accept any challenge, and overcome any obstacle. This is because it is rooted within principles derived from the Qur\u2019an and Sunnah. While these principles are stable and secure, they are by no means rigid and stiff. On the contrary, they are flexible and applicable to real-life scenarios of all types. Islamic jurisprudence is like a tree; firmly grounded and unshakable, yet its branches reach far and wide, providing shade and fruits to all those around it. The evolution of Islamic Finance is a prime example of this adaptability. This article will explore the inception of Islamic Finance and its nature. Subsequent articles will discuss the principles that govern Islamic Finance and a practical application of these principles on Islamic Finance products. In doing so, this series of articles will show the genius of Islamic scholarship in the last century.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

A brief glimpse into the inception of Islamic Finance<\/strong><\/h6>\n\n\n\n

Finance is not a subject foreign to Islamic jurisprudence. On account of being social creatures, humans often turn to others when in need. More often than not, we find that an individual in need of money will request another who has a surplus of money for a loan. Interest-based loans take advantage of this weakness, with the wealthy ready to lend money immediately, but obligating more in return, effectively creating money out of nothing. Our tradition has provided a much more ethical alternative to such financing in the form of qar\u1e0dh \u1e25asan <\/em>(benevolent loan), in which loans are given without any excess to be expected in return. These ethical loans are not meant to cheat people of their wealth, rather they are initiated as voluntary contributions in which compensation is sought only from Allah. Additionally, finance in the earlier days differed from finance today in another manner; it was not institutionalized in the form of banks, rather it was done on an individual basis. As a result of these two factors, financing in Muslim communities was never meant for profit. It was a means of assisting each other and seeking Allah\u2019s pleasure. In the wake of colonialism, Muslims found themselves within a different environment. Alongside the many religious, cultural, and political changes, the economic consequences of colonialism and imperialism manifested in the forms of banks, factories, and insurance companies. Also, due to industrialization, money was found at a higher concentration than ever before. As a result, individual and ethical financing, built upon assisting each other and seeking Allah\u2019s pleasure, was overshadowed by institutionalized and profit-based financing, a system grounded within interest and satiating one\u2019s endless greed.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

In a series of lessons on Islamic Finance, Shaikh Taha Karaan (May Allah have mercy upon him) notes that although institutionalized financing took a turn for the worst, financing isn\u2019t inherently<\/strong> evil. The issue lies within cheating others and using interest to create money out of nothing. Regardless, institutionalized financing had its benefits as well. Individual financing was effective within small groups, amongst friends, and within families. A large-scale project however would be impractical to manage. Institutional financing made this possible while also providing a higher degree of security and stability. Yes, the benefits were many, but was it possible to reap these benefits without the interest-based system institutionalized finance was built upon? Could finance be purified of its unethical and impermissible form whilst retaining its productive and effective nature? Islamic finance was initiated as the answer to these questions and the solution to these problems. <\/p>\n\n\n\n

The vision of Islamic finance was first implemented by Ahmed al-Najj\u0101r in Mit Ghamr, a town in Egypt, in the year 1963. Over half a century later, Islamic finance has grown exponentially and has made its way into Muslim and non-Muslim countries alike. However, many Muslims still find themselves hesitant to accept this relatively new venture. Some are held back by fear of change and the unknown. Others are simply too lazy to bother with it. Many truly have their reservations: What is the difference between Islamic Finance and conventional finance? They both earn the same profit, or worse, Islamic institutions charge more! Will changing a few documents and adding on a few Arabic words really make interest lawful? By raising awareness and spreading knowledge, we at Talweeh believe that the current fear, indifference, and doubts can be transformed into confidence, engagement, and clarity. We will now discuss the nature of Islamic Finance and the default approach to transactions. As a disclaimer, we are not arguing that all Islamic institutions uphold these principles. We are simply presenting the nature of Islamic Finance and the theory that governs it.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

The nature of Islamic Finance<\/strong><\/h6>\n\n\n\n

As mentioned previously, traditional academia did not discuss institutionalized finance as it did individual finance. However, trade, commerce, and an array of transactions were plentiful within our tradition. When pressed to provide an alternative for the Muslim community, expert financiers joined hands with genius scholars to give rise to Islamic Finance. After researching the objectives of institutionalized finance, the different chapters of trade and commerce were blended to fulfill these objectives. Transactions that were initially used for trade and commerce were altered to be used for finance. Since different chapters of Fiqh were at play, a high degree of caution was required to ensure that the conditions of each chapter remained intact, otherwise resulting in the invalidity of the transaction.
